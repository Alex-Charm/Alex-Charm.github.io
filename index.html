A nation where every
stone tells a story

France is the largest country in the European Union, stretching from the English Channel to the Mediterranean Sea. With a history spanning from Celtic tribes to modern republics, it has been the cradle of the Enlightenment, the birthplace of human rights, and the world's most visited destination for over three decades.

Its diverse landscapes — alpine peaks, rolling vineyards, rugged coastlines, and lavender-scented plains — mirror the extraordinary diversity of its culture, language, and traditions across 13 metropolitan regions.

La Marseillaise

Written in 1792 by Claude Joseph Rouget de Lisle in Strasbourg, La Marseillaise is the anthem of revolution, freedom, and the indomitable French spirit.

La Marseillaise

Claude Joseph Rouget de Lisle, 1792

0:00 0:42

Composed on the night of April 25–26, 1792 during the French Revolutionary Wars

Originally called "Chant de guerre pour l'Armée du Rhin" — renamed after volunteers from Marseille sang it

Declared the official national anthem on July 14, 1795, banned under Napoleon III, restored in 1879

Gastronomie

The Art of
French Cuisine

French gastronomy is a UNESCO-recognized intangible cultural heritage. From the humble baguette to elaborate Michelin-starred menus, food in France is an art form and a source of immense national pride.

Each region boasts its own specialties: bouillabaisse in Marseille, coq au vin in Burgundy, quiche in Lorraine, crêpes in Brittany, and ratatouille in Provence.
